## Persona 1 — Dev Dana
### "I just want it to work without reading a 40-page contract."

---

### 1. Demographics and Context

**Role:** Software engineer (IC or lead) or startup CTO at a seed–Series A company, 5–50 person team.

**Company type:** SaaS, marketplace, e-commerce, logistics platform, healthcare, or real estate tech. Any product that collects or displays a US address as part of its core flow.

**Team size:** 1–5 engineers. No dedicated data team. Dana is the person who researches, evaluates, and integrates third-party APIs.

**A typical day — three moments this problem surfaces:**
1. **Sprint planning:** PMs add "address validation" to the backlog for the 4th time. Dana knows they have to deal with it eventually and dread the research.
2. **Integration attempt:** Dana signs up for SmartyStreets trial, hits the enrichment paywall, discovers census tract requires a separate product, starts Googling alternatives.
3. **Post-launch:** A customer complains their address was rejected even though it's real. Dana opens a support ticket and realizes the API they chose has coverage gaps in FL or CA.

---

### 2. Psychographics

**Core fear:** Picking the wrong vendor and being stuck in a contract that doesn't solve the problem, or being dependent on a brittle API with no docs and no SLA.

**Core desire:** To integrate address validation in half a day and never think about it again. To get enrichment fields (census, flood zone) bundled in — not as a gotcha on the next invoice.

**Self-identity:** "I build things that work." Dana takes pride in picking well-documented, reliable tools. They dislike complexity theater and vendor lock-in. They want to explain the decision to their CTO in one sentence.

---

### 3. Jobs-to-Be-Done

**Functional:** Validate and standardize US addresses at signup or checkout. Optionally enrich with census tract for analytics, flood zone for compliance, or timezone for scheduling.

**Emotional:** Feel confident the API will "just work" for all 50 states, not discover gaps 6 months after launch when a customer complains.

**Social:** Be seen as the engineer who makes pragmatic, high-quality vendor choices — not someone who picks the cheapest option and creates tech debt.

---

### 4. Purchase Triggers and Barriers

**Triggers:**
1. Assigned an "address validation" ticket with a 2-week deadline
2. Current approach (regex or Google Maps API) fails QA — too many false negatives on real addresses
3. Someone on Hacker News posts "Show HN: address API with FEMA flood zone included" — Dana opens the tab

**Objections:**
1. *"How's the coverage? SmartyStreets said they had full coverage and then FL addresses failed."* — need state-level coverage data upfront
2. *"What happens when you go down? We have no fallback."* — need uptime numbers and status page
3. *"The free tier is 1K/day — is that per key or per account? Can I test without hitting a wall?"* — pricing ambiguity stalls signup

**Decision influencers:** Dana decides alone for initial integration. If monthly cost exceeds $200, CTO signs off in a 5-min Slack thread. Procurement is not involved at this stage.

---

### 5. Channels and Content

**Where Dana finds solutions:**
- **RapidAPI** — first stop for API discovery; reads reviews, compares request/response format in the UI before signing up
- **Hacker News** — Show HN posts and Ask HN "what address API do you use?" threads
- **Google** — "SmartyStreets alternative", "US address validation API free tier", "FEMA flood zone API"
- **Dev.to / Stack Overflow** — integration questions, comparison posts
- **GitHub** — reads the repo if one is linked; lack of public repo raises suspicion

**Content that converts (not just engages):**
- A real API response JSON with all enrichment fields visible — no sales copy needed
- A curl command that works in under 30 seconds
- A comparison table showing what's included at each tier, including what competitors charge separately
- A /docs page with copy-paste examples in Node.js and Python (Dana's two most likely stacks)
- A status page with real uptime numbers (not "99.9% SLA" marketing)

---

### 6. The Message That Closes the Deal

> **"One API call. Address validation + census tract + FEMA flood zone + timezone. Free tier, no credit card."**

**The single word that must not be missing:** **free** — Dana will not spend 20 minutes integrating an API without a free tier that lets them test in production.

## Persona 2 — Compliance Claire
### "I need the flood zone determination. My legal team needs it. That's it."

---

### 1. Demographics and Context

**Role:** Product Manager, VP of Engineering, or Head of Data/Compliance at a PropTech, fintech (mortgage origination, HELOC, insurance underwriting), or InsurTech company.

**Company size:** 10–500 employees. Has a legal/compliance team. Has a data vendor review process but it's not as slow as enterprise procurement.

**A typical day — three moments this problem surfaces:**
1. **Compliance review:** Legal flags that the loan origination flow is missing NFIP flood zone determination. Claire is assigned to "find a solution by end of quarter."
2. **Vendor evaluation:** Claire calls SmartyStreets. They don't offer flood zone. She calls Melissa. Sales wants a 30-minute call before sending pricing. She Googles "FEMA flood zone API self-serve" in frustration.
3. **HMDA audit prep:** Compliance team needs census tract on every loan file. Current process is manual CSV lookup against Census Bureau website. It takes 3 hours per batch of 500 loans.

---

### 2. Psychographics

**Core fear:** Shipping a product that passes internal QA but fails a regulatory audit. The fear is not about the API — it's about the downstream consequence: fines, legal exposure, delayed product launch, or personal accountability.

**Core desire:** A reliable, auditable data source that returns the same flood zone code as the official FEMA NFHL — so she can tell legal "we use FEMA's own data, served through a clean API."

**Self-identity:** "I get compliance problems solved without slowing down the product team." Claire bridges engineering and legal. She is evaluated on shipping compliant products on time, not on unit cost.

---

### 3. Jobs-to-Be-Done

**Functional:** Determine FEMA flood zone (AE, X, etc.) and `flood_sfha` flag for every property address processed in the mortgage or insurance workflow. Return census tract + FIPS for HMDA reporting. Do this without a manual lookup process.

**Emotional:** Feel confident that if a regulator asks "where does your flood zone data come from?" she can say "FEMA NFHL API, served via GeoClear" and it will hold up.

**Social:** Be seen by the CTO and legal team as someone who solves compliance blockers fast, with vendor choices that don't introduce new risk.

---

### 4. Purchase Triggers and Barriers

**Triggers:**
1. Legal or compliance team sends a written request for flood zone determination capability
2. Product team launches mortgage/insurance feature and compliance checklist blocks it
3. An industry peer at a PropTech conference mentions they built a flood determination workflow for $249/mo — Claire searches for it on Monday

**Objections:**
1. *"Is this the actual FEMA data or something derived? We need the primary source."* — Claire needs to know GeoClear is calling FEMA NFHL directly, not a derived dataset
2. *"What's your data refresh frequency? FEMA updates flood maps quarterly."* — she needs to know how stale the data can be
3. *"Do you have a BAA / data processing agreement we can sign?"* — compliance teams at regulated companies may need a contractual data lineage document

**Decision influencers:** Claire decides the vendor and tier. Legal reviews the data source (one conversation). Finance approves the spend (< $500/mo is usually pre-approved budget). No RFP at this price point.

---

### 5. Channels and Content

**Where Claire finds solutions:**
- **Google** — "FEMA flood zone API", "NFIP flood determination API", "census tract API HMDA compliance"
- **LinkedIn** — industry groups for PropTech, mortgage tech, InsurTech; vendor recommendations in comments
- **Direct outreach** — if GeoClear emails her with the right subject line ("flood zone determination API, self-serve, $249/mo"), she opens it. The compliance problem is pre-existing and she's been meaning to solve it.
- **Peer referrals** — "who do you use for flood zone?" is a question that gets asked at mortgage tech meetups and in Slack groups

**Content that converts:**
- A sample API response showing `flood_zone: "AE"` and `flood_sfha: true` — the compliance team cares about those exact fields
- A clear statement that data is sourced from FEMA NFHL API (with link to FEMA's documentation)
- A comparison: "GeoClear vs manual FEMA lookup vs SmartyStreets (no flood zone)" — Claire needs to explain the decision up the chain
- A /compliance or /mortgage landing page variant that speaks her language (NFIP, HMDA, FIPS, census tract) before mentioning developer features

---

### 6. The Message That Closes the Deal

> **"FEMA flood zone + census tract, every US address, self-serve pricing. No sales call. No enterprise contract."**

**The single word that must not be missing:** **compliance** — Claire's entire purchase is driven by a compliance requirement. If the word doesn't appear, the product doesn't register as the solution to her problem.

## Persona 3 — Ops Owen
### "We have 2 million customer records and half the addresses are garbage."

---

### 1. Demographics and Context

**Role:** VP of Operations, Director of Data Quality, or CRM Administrator at a mid-market company (50–500 employees) in logistics, e-commerce, insurance, healthcare, or field services.

**Team size:** Ops team of 3–15. Uses Salesforce, HubSpot, or a custom CRM. Has a quarterly data quality initiative that never quite gets finished.

**A typical day — three moments this problem surfaces:**
1. **Returned shipments:** Logistics coordinator flags that 3.2% of deliveries failed last month due to bad addresses. Owen pulls a report and sees $40K in re-delivery costs.
2. **CRM deduplication project:** Marketing team wants to run a direct mail campaign. The list has 200K addresses. Owen knows at least 15% are undeliverable but has no way to clean them at scale.
3. **Field service routing:** Dispatch sends a technician to an address that doesn't exist in the routing software. Technician calls in. This happens 12 times a week.

---

### 2. Psychographics

**Core fear:** Wasting budget — on returned shipments, on failed mail campaigns, on field service no-shows — because of data quality problems that "aren't really my job" but keep landing on their desk.

**Core desire:** A one-time bulk job that cleans the existing record set, plus a validation check on every new address at entry so the problem doesn't compound. Never have to think about address quality again.

**Self-identity:** "I make operations run without drama." Owen is pragmatic. They are not a developer but they can read documentation and run a CSV upload if the UX is clear. They evaluate tools on ROI, not elegance.

---

### 3. Jobs-to-Be-Done

**Functional:** Validate a backlog of 10K–2M addresses in bulk. Flag undeliverable addresses. Standardize format. Optionally enrich with RDI flag to separate residential from commercial for routing.

**Emotional:** Feel like the address quality problem is solved and won't surface as a support ticket or exec report again.

**Social:** Quantify the fix — "we reduced returned shipments by 2.8%" — and present it to the VP of Finance as operational savings.

---

### 4. Purchase Triggers and Barriers

**Triggers:**
1. A monthly ops review where returned shipment cost is called out by Finance
2. A failed direct mail campaign where bounce rate is traced back to bad address data
3. A competitor or peer mentions using an address validation API to clean their CRM before a campaign

**Objections:**
1. *"We have 500K records. Can your bulk API handle that, or do I need a developer to write a script?"* — Owen needs a no-code or low-code path (CSV upload → enriched CSV download)
2. *"How much will this cost? I need to put a number in a budget request."* — needs clear per-address pricing for metered tier, or a flat rate they can tie to the project scope
3. *"What if the address exists but the suite/unit is wrong? Does the API tell me that?"* — needs to understand what "validated" actually means in terms of confidence score

**Decision influencers:** Owen makes the recommendation. Finance approves spend < $1K/mo. IT reviews data handling (no PII stored by GeoClear). Decision time: 1–3 weeks if budget is pre-approved, 4–8 weeks if a new budget request is needed.

---

### 5. Channels and Content

**Where Owen finds solutions:**
- **G2 / Capterra** — "address verification software" category; reads reviews focused on bulk processing and ROI
- **Google** — "bulk address validation API", "address verification for CRM cleanup", "CSV address validation tool"
- **Software vendor ecosystem** — Salesforce AppExchange, HubSpot integrations marketplace
- **Peer referrals** — ops leaders in industry associations (NRF for retail, NAIOP for proptech, etc.)

**Content that converts:**
- A CSV upload demo or walkthrough video showing input → enriched output in under 2 minutes
- A clear explanation of the `confidence` score (0–100) and what to do with low-confidence results
- A per-address cost calculator — "500K addresses × $0.002 = $1,000 one-time cleanup"
- A case study or testimonial from a logistics or e-commerce ops team (even one is enough)
- Integration guide for Salesforce or HubSpot (even if it's just a Zapier step)

---

### 6. The Message That Closes the Deal

> **"Upload your address list. Get back validated, enriched addresses. Per-address pricing — no subscription required."**

**The single word that must not be missing:** **bulk** — Owen's problem is a backlog, not a real-time integration. If the product doesn't visibly solve batch/bulk, it's not their solution.

## Cross-Persona Insights

### Conversion sequence (most likely first paying customer)
1. **Dev Dana** — lowest friction; self-serve free tier → trial → Starter upgrade. Volume: many. ACV: low. Effort: near-zero (RapidAPI listing + HN post triggers inbound).
2. **Compliance Claire** — highest ACV per customer; warm outreach converts if the message leads with NFIP/flood zone. Volume: fewer. Effort: one targeted email per company.
3. **Ops Owen** — medium ACV; currently blocked by missing CSV upload UI. Once built, G2 listing + Capterra becomes the channel. Effort: product work required first.

### Shared objection: data source credibility
All three personas ask "where does this data come from?" before purchasing. The answer is already strong:
- Address data: USDOT National Address Database (federal open data) + Overture Maps
- Flood zone: FEMA NFHL API (live, primary source — not a derived dataset)
- Census tract: US Census Bureau Geocoder API (live, primary source)

This needs to be visible on the landing page and in every outreach email — not buried in docs.

### Pricing fit
- Dana: free tier lowers the barrier to trial. Starter ($49) is the first conversion. Pro ($249) is the enrichment unlock.
- Claire: jumps straight to Pro ($249) — compliance justification makes the price irrelevant. Potential Enterprise for audit trail + SLA needs.
- Owen: metered tier (pay-per-lookup) is ideal for one-time bulk cleanup projects. Starter/Pro for ongoing validation at entry.
